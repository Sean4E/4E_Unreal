Outdoor Environments in UE5

Understand the landscape system, what it does, and what you will build across these eight steps.

00

Outdoor Environments in UE5

What is the Landscape system? UE5 provides a dedicated system for creating large-scale outdoor terrain. Unlike placing individual static meshes to form ground geometry, the Landscape system uses a heightmap-based approach — a grid of vertices that you push up or pull down to shape hills, valleys, and mountains. It is purpose-built for natural environments.

Landscapes vs Static Meshes

ApproachBest ForHow It Works
LandscapeLarge outdoor terrain (hills, fields, mountains)Heightmap grid — sculpt and paint directly in the editor
Static MeshesIndividual objects (rocks, buildings, props)Pre-made 3D models placed into the scene
Think of a Landscape as a sheet of clay you can push and pull with your fingers. Static Meshes are pre-made objects you place on top of that clay — a rock, a tree, a building. You use both together to build a complete outdoor environment.

Key Landscape Features

  • Sculpting — raise, lower, smooth, flatten, and erode terrain directly in the viewport
  • Painting — apply multiple material layers (grass, dirt, rock) by painting onto the surface
  • Foliage — scatter trees, grass, and plants across the landscape with a brush
  • Atmosphere — sky, clouds, fog, and lighting systems designed for outdoor scenes

What We Will Build

Over these eight steps, you will create a complete natural outdoor environment:

  • A sculpted terrain with hills, valleys, and a flat area
  • A multi-layer painted landscape material (grass, dirt, rock)
  • A realistic sky with volumetric clouds and atmospheric fog
  • Foliage scattered across the landscape (trees, grass, bushes)
  • A water body (lake or river)
  • Props, sound, and final atmospheric polish
Milestone: You understand the difference between Landscapes and Static Meshes, and you know what features the Landscape system provides.
01

Create a Landscape

The first step is to create the terrain itself. UE5 has a dedicated Landscape Mode that lets you configure and generate a heightmap-based terrain with a single click.

Enter Landscape Mode

  1. Open your UE5 project (or create a new one with the Blank template)
  2. Press Shift+3 to enter Landscape Mode (or use the Modes dropdown in the toolbar)
  3. You will see the Landscape panel appear on the left with Manage, Sculpt, and Paint tabs
  4. Make sure Manage → New Landscape is selected

Configure Landscape Settings

These three settings control the resolution and size of your terrain grid:

SettingWhat It ControlsBeginner Value
Section SizeQuads per landscape section63 x 63
Sections Per ComponentHow many sections make up one component1 x 1
Number of ComponentsTotal components across the landscape8 x 8
Beginner recommendation: Use 63x63, 1x1, 8x8. This creates a manageable terrain that is large enough to build an interesting environment but small enough to work with comfortably. You can always create a larger landscape later.

Create the Terrain

  1. Leave the Scale at 100, 100, 100 (default)
  2. Click the Create button at the bottom of the Landscape panel
  3. A flat green terrain will appear in the Viewport
  4. Navigate around it: hold Right-click + WASD to fly, scroll to adjust speed
  5. Press F with the landscape selected to focus on it and get a sense of scale
Note: Once created, you cannot change the component count. If you need a bigger landscape later, you will need to delete and re-create it. Plan your size before clicking Create.
Milestone: You have a flat landscape in your Viewport. You can fly around it and see the green default material covering the surface.
02

Sculpt the Terrain

Now you will shape the flat terrain into something natural. The Sculpt tab in Landscape Mode provides several tools for pushing, pulling, smoothing, and eroding the heightmap.

Sculpt Tools Overview

ToolWhat It DoesWhen to Use
SculptRaises terrain where you paintCreating hills and elevated areas
SmoothAverages the height of nearby verticesRemoving jagged edges and harsh transitions
FlattenSets all vertices to the same heightCreating flat plateaus or building sites
ErosionSimulates natural weatheringAdding realistic erosion to cliff faces
NoiseAdds random variation to the heightBreaking up uniform areas with natural roughness

Brush Settings

Every sculpt tool uses a circular brush. You control three properties:

  • Brush Size — radius of the brush. Use [ and ] keys to resize quickly
  • Brush Falloff — how soft the edge of the brush is (0.0 = hard edge, 1.0 = feathered)
  • Tool Strength — how much effect each stroke has (keep this low, around 0.1 to 0.3)
Key tip: Hold Shift while sculpting to lower the terrain instead of raising it. This works with the Sculpt tool and is essential for creating valleys and river beds.

Sculpting Techniques

  1. Start with a large brush and low strength (0.1–0.2) — this creates gentle, natural shapes
  2. Create some rolling hills: paint broad strokes across the terrain with the Sculpt tool
  3. Hold Shift and sculpt to carve a valley between two hills
  4. Use the Smooth tool to soften any harsh edges or unnatural peaks
  5. Leave a flat area for placing a lake or building later
  6. Switch to a smaller brush and add detail: subtle ridges, dips, cliff edges
  7. Use Erosion on steep slopes to add realistic weathering
  8. Use Noise sparingly on flat areas to break up uniformity
Think of sculpting in two passes: the first pass is broad strokes with a large, soft brush to establish the overall landforms. The second pass is detail work with a smaller brush — adding character, roughness, and interest to specific areas.
Common mistake: Using too much strength creates spiky, unnatural terrain. Keep strength low and build up gradually. You can always add more, but flattening and smoothing takes time.
Milestone: Your terrain has varied elevation — rolling hills, at least one valley, and a flat area. It looks like natural land, not a flat plane.

Materials, Sky & Atmosphere

Give your terrain colour and texture, then build the sky above it.

03

Paint the Landscape

A sculpted landscape still looks flat and featureless without materials. UE5 lets you paint multiple material layers directly onto the terrain — grass on flat areas, dirt on paths, rock on steep slopes. This is done through a special Landscape Material with blendable layers.

Create a Landscape Material

  1. In the Content Browser, right-click → Material → name it M_Landscape
  2. Double-click to open the Material Editor (a node-based graph)
  3. Right-click in the graph → search for Landscape Layer Blend → add it
  4. In the Details Panel of the Landscape Layer Blend node, click + to add layers
  5. Add three layers: Grass, Dirt, Rock
  6. Set each layer’s Blend Type to LB Weight Blend

Connect Textures to Each Layer

For each layer, you need a colour texture and a normal map:

  1. Right-click in the graph → Texture Sample → assign a grass texture to the Texture property
  2. Create another Texture Sample for the grass normal map
  3. Repeat for dirt and rock (6 Texture Sample nodes total: 3 colour + 3 normal)
  4. Connect the colour outputs to the Landscape Layer Blend inputs
  5. Create a second Landscape Layer Blend for normals and connect the normal maps
  6. Wire the first Blend output to Base Color and the second to Normal
  7. Click Apply and Save
Where to get textures: Open Fab (formerly Quixel Bridge) from Window → Fab. Search for grass, soil, and rock surface textures. Download and they appear in your Content Browser.

Assign and Paint

  1. Select the Landscape in the Viewport
  2. In the Details Panel, find the Landscape Material slot and assign M_Landscape
  3. Switch to the Paint tab in Landscape Mode
  4. You will see your layers listed: Grass, Dirt, Rock
  5. Select a layer and paint directly onto the terrain with the brush
  6. Paint Grass on flat open areas
  7. Paint Dirt along paths, valleys, and transition areas
  8. Paint Rock on steep cliff faces and exposed surfaces

Auto-Paint by Slope (Advanced)

You can set the material to automatically apply rock on steep slopes and grass on flat areas:

  • In the Material Editor, add a Landscape Layer Sample node
  • Use a World Aligned Blend or slope-based logic to blend layers
  • Steep angles (above ~45 degrees) receive the Rock layer, flat areas receive Grass
  • This gives you a natural base that you can then refine by hand-painting
Think of auto-painting like a smart fill: the engine looks at the slope of each point on the terrain and applies the appropriate texture. You then paint over specific areas to add paths, clearings, or detail.
Milestone: Your terrain has a multi-layer material applied. Grass covers flat areas, rock appears on slopes, and dirt marks transitions. The landscape looks like real ground.
04

Sky & Atmosphere

An outdoor landscape needs a sky. UE5 provides a full atmospheric rendering system that simulates realistic sky colour, sun position, clouds, and distance haze — all without any external tools or skybox textures.

Add Atmosphere Actors

Place these actors into your level from the Place Actors panel (or type their names into the search):

  1. Sky Atmosphere — generates the realistic sky dome with Rayleigh scattering
  2. Directional Light — acts as the sun. Rotate it to change time of day
  3. Sky Light — captures ambient light from the sky and applies it as fill lighting
  4. Exponential Height Fog — adds distance haze and atmospheric depth
  5. Volumetric Clouds — renders a full 3D cloud layer in the sky
  6. Post Process Volume — controls colour grading, exposure, bloom, and other visual effects

Configure Each Actor

Directional Light (Sun)

  • Check Atmosphere Sun Light in the Details Panel
  • Rotate on the Y axis to change the sun’s elevation
  • Low angles = sunrise/sunset (warm golden light)
  • High angles = midday (bright, harsh)
  • Below horizon = nighttime

Sky Light

  • Set Real Time Capture to enabled
  • This makes the Sky Light update dynamically as you move the sun
  • The ambient light colour will match the sky automatically
  • Adjust Intensity to control fill light brightness

Exponential Height Fog

  • Adds atmospheric depth — distant objects fade into haze
  • Increase Fog Density for thick mist, decrease for a clear day
  • Enable Volumetric Fog for light shafts (god rays) through trees and clouds
  • Adjust Fog Inscattering Color to match your sky’s colour mood

Post Process Volume

  • Place a Post Process Volume and set Infinite Extent (Unbound) to true in the Details Panel
  • Exposure: set Min/Max Brightness to control how bright or dark the scene adapts
  • Bloom: adds a soft glow around bright areas (keep subtle, around 0.5)
  • Color Grading: use Saturation, Contrast, and White Balance to set your mood
  • Vignette: darkens edges for a cinematic feel (0.2–0.4 is usually enough)

05

Foliage & Trees

An empty terrain feels lifeless. The Foliage Mode lets you scatter thousands of meshes (trees, grass, bushes, flowers) across the landscape with a paint brush. UE5 handles instancing automatically, so you can place dense vegetation while keeping performance reasonable.

Enter Foliage Mode

  1. Press Shift+4 to enter Foliage Mode (or use the Modes dropdown)
  2. The Foliage panel appears with a list of foliage types and brush settings
  3. Click + Add Foliage Type (the + button) to add meshes
  4. Drag a Static Mesh from the Content Browser into the foliage list (e.g. a tree mesh from Fab)
  5. Repeat for different vegetation types: grass clumps, bushes, flowers

Paint Foliage

  1. Select one or more foliage types in the list (checkmark them)
  2. Adjust the Brush Size — large for broad coverage, small for detail
  3. Click and drag on the landscape to paint foliage instances
  4. Hold Shift while painting to erase foliage
  5. Add trees on hilltops and along ridges
  6. Add grass and ground cover in open areas
  7. Add bushes along the edges of paths and near water

Foliage Settings per Type

Click on a foliage type in the list and expand its settings:

  • Density — how many instances per brush stroke. Higher = denser vegetation
  • Scale Min / Max — random size variation (e.g. 0.8 to 1.2 for natural variety)
  • Align to Surface — tilts meshes to match the terrain slope
  • Random Yaw — randomises rotation so instances don’t all face the same way
  • Collision — enable for trees (so the camera doesn’t clip through them), disable for grass (performance)

Where to Get Foliage Assets

  • Fab (formerly Quixel Megascans): thousands of free photorealistic trees, grass, and plants. Open Window → Fab in UE5
  • Starter Content: basic vegetation meshes if you enabled it during project creation
  • Search for: Meadow Pack, Tropical Jungle Pack, European Vegetation on the Fab marketplace
Procedural placement: Right-click a foliage type → Fill to automatically scatter instances across the entire landscape. Then erase from areas where you don’t want them (paths, water, building sites).
Performance tip: Grass meshes can create millions of instances. Keep grass foliage collision disabled and use LOD (Level of Detail) meshes. Start sparse and add more if performance allows.
Milestone: Your landscape has trees, grass, and bushes scattered naturally across the terrain. The scene is starting to feel like a real place.
06

Water

UE5 has a built-in Water system that provides ready-made water body actors: oceans, rivers, and lakes. These come with realistic rendering, wave animation, and automatic landscape carving — the terrain lowers itself where water is placed.

Water Body Actors

ActorWhat It CreatesBest For
Water Body OceanInfinite ocean plane with wavesCoastal or island landscapes
Water Body RiverSpline-based flowing waterStreams, rivers through valleys
Water Body LakeEnclosed body of still waterPonds, lakes in terrain depressions

Place a Water Body Lake

  1. Before placing water, use the Sculpt tool to lower the terrain where the lake will sit — create a natural depression
  2. In the Place Actors panel, search for Water Body Lake
  3. Drag it into the Viewport and position it over your depression
  4. You will see a spline (a path with control points) defining the lake shape
  5. Select individual spline points and move them (W key) to shape the lake outline
  6. Alt+click on the spline to add new control points for more complex shapes
  7. Adjust the water level by moving the actor up or down on the Z axis

Water Properties

  • Water Color: adjust in the Water Material to match your environment (deep blue, murky green, clear turquoise)
  • Opacity: controls how transparent the water is — shallow water should be more transparent
  • Wave Speed / Height: controls surface animation (calm lake = low values)
  • Auto-carve: the landscape automatically lowers where water overlaps — you may need to manually blend the shoreline
Tip: Paint the landscape material around the shoreline with Dirt or a wet sand texture to create a natural transition from water to land.
Note: The Water plugin must be enabled. Go to Edit → Plugins, search for Water, and ensure it is checked. A restart may be required.
Milestone: Your landscape has a body of water that sits naturally in the terrain. The shoreline blends with the surrounding ground material.
07

Props & Polish

The final step is adding detail and atmosphere. A landscape with terrain, materials, foliage, and water is a solid foundation — but what makes it feel alive are the small details: scattered rocks, environmental sound, particle effects, and careful lighting adjustments.

Place Props & Rocks

  1. Download rock and stone assets from Fab — search for “rock” or “boulder”
  2. Drag rocks into the scene along cliff edges, shorelines, and paths
  3. Vary the scale and rotation of each rock so they look natural
  4. Press the End key to snap props down to the terrain surface
  5. Add structures if desired: a cabin, bridge, ruins, fences

Decals for Blending

Decals are flat textures projected onto surfaces. Use them to blend props into the environment:

  • Place dirt decals at the base of rocks so they look embedded in the ground
  • Place leaf litter decals under trees to simulate fallen foliage
  • Place moss decals on rock faces and old structures
  • In the Content Browser, right-click → Material → set Material Domain to Deferred Decal

Particle Effects

Add subtle motion to the environment using Niagara particle systems:

  • Dust motes — small floating particles lit by the sun
  • Fireflies — tiny emissive dots for twilight or night scenes
  • Falling leaves — spawned near trees and drifting downward
  • Mist — low ground fog near water or in valleys
  • Find pre-made Niagara effects on Fab, or create simple ones in the Niagara Editor

Ambient Sound

  1. In Place Actors, search for Ambient Sound
  2. Drag it into the scene
  3. In the Details Panel, assign a sound asset: wind, birds chirping, water flowing
  4. Set Attenuation to control how far the sound carries
  5. Place multiple sounds: wind on hilltops, water near the lake, birds near trees
Tip: UE5 Starter Content includes basic ambient sounds. For more variety, check Fab for environment audio packs.

✓

Final Checklist

Before you call your landscape finished, verify each element:

  • Sculpted terrain with varied elevation — hills, valleys, and at least one flat area
  • Painted landscape material with multiple layers (grass, dirt, rock) blended naturally
  • Sky Atmosphere with Directional Light acting as the sun and Volumetric Clouds
  • Sky Light set to Real Time Capture for dynamic ambient lighting
  • Exponential Height Fog adding atmospheric depth and distance haze
  • Foliage placed naturally — trees, grass, bushes with scale variation
  • Water body (if applicable) — lake, river, or ocean with blended shoreline
  • Props and environmental details — rocks, structures, decals at prop bases
  • Particle effects — dust, leaves, mist for subtle environmental motion
  • Ambient sound — wind, birds, water for audio atmosphere
  • Post Process Volume — colour grading, exposure, bloom set for your mood
  • Atmospheric mood — lighting, fog, and post-process working together
